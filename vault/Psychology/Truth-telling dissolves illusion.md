## Core idea

When a person begins speaking from alignment rather than adaptation, grief often appears because clarity dissolves the imagined version of the future that once sustained hope.

## Structure

For long periods people may adapt their needs to maintain relational stability, holding onto possibilities that depend on the other person eventually changing or becoming ready. When truth is finally spoken without minimizing those needs, the internal illusion that supported the relationship collapses. The grief that follows is not necessarily about losing the person themselves but about losing the imagined trajectory of the relationship. Clarity therefore produces both relief and mourning, because truth reveals limits that hope previously blurred.

## Human signal

Grief in these moments is not evidence of error. It is the emotional process of releasing a story that once protected the heart.
