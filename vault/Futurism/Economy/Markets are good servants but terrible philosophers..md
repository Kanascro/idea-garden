## Core idea

Markets are useful as bounded tools for experimentation, procurement, and modular competition. They fail when allowed to define value, dignity, or the shape of a good life.

## Structure

Markets work best at the edges: supplying known components, testing nonessential forms, and rewarding durability, transparency, and craft under rules set elsewhere. Essentials such as care, infrastructure, cultural legitimacy, and public software governance must be structured outside market logic. The key boundary is not public versus private, but whether failure is survivable and whether market participation can shape status, safety, or belonging. Once something becomes essential, formative, or system-critical, it should leave the market layer.

## Human signal

The desired feeling is that markets become boring on purpose: dry, technical servants rather than engines of meaning. Variety and play remain, but they no longer get to decide who matters.
