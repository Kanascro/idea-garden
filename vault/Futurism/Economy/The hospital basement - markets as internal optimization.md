## Core idea

In essential systems such as healthcare, markets can exist only as internal procurement mechanisms serving predefined public goals rather than shaping the system itself.

## Structure

The hospital’s primary function—care—is universal and removed from market logic. Patients are not customers, physicians are salaried professionals accountable to peer review, and treatment is guided by medical ethics rather than pricing signals. Markets appear only in the operational basement: procurement of standardized inputs such as gloves, imaging parts, bed hardware, and software modules. Suppliers compete to provide components that meet externally defined specifications. Selection criteria prioritize reliability, durability, maintenance history, and safety over simple price. All performance data are public, and suppliers who compromise quality are blacklisted across the public system. The market therefore performs a technical role: allocating suppliers for known needs under strict oversight.

## Human signal

The atmosphere of this market is intentionally dull. It is not a theatre of innovation or prestige but a quiet logistical mechanism ensuring that care functions smoothly above it.
