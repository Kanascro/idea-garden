## Core idea

The problem is not status-seeking itself but the social rewards attached to it. A humane society works only if admiration detaches from accumulation and reattaches to contribution, mastery, stewardship, and responsibility. Human beings always create status hierarchies, but societies can decide which behaviors status rewards.

## Structure

Rules against logos, ads, or prestige signaling cannot hold if wealth still functions as the main proxy for power and respect. Under insecurity, people chase domination; under security, they more often chase excellence, recognition, and meaningful differentiation. In a bounded market order, competition remains, but as craft, experimentation, and peer regard rather than extraction or enclosure. Marketing then shrinks into discovery and functional signaling, because its manipulative force depends on fear, inadequacy, and unstable status.

Attempts to eliminate status-seeking fail because the drive for recognition predates both markets and states. People naturally develop symbolic hierarchies reflecting admired traits. The central design question therefore becomes which behaviors receive admiration. If wealth, dominance, and visibility function as status signals, individuals will find ways to encode those signals even under regulatory constraints. When societies instead reward craftsmanship, generosity, wisdom, reliability, or stewardship, prestige aligns with socially beneficial actions. Status remains, but its incentives change.
## Human signal

This view is unsentimental: people do not become morally pure, and cheating never vanishes. But the system stops feeding predators with jackpots, and ambition becomes directional instead of devouring.
