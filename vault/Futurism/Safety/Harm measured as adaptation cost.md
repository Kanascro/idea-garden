## Core idea

The severity of harm can be understood through the long-term adaptation cost it imposes on a person’s life rather than solely through the event itself.

## Structure

Different individuals experience the same event with dramatically different long-term impacts. What matters is how much ongoing energy the nervous system must spend protecting, avoiding, or compensating afterward. When fear, vigilance, or coping strategies consume large portions of a person’s attention and freedom, the adaptation cost is high. When the nervous system can quickly return to equilibrium, the cost is low. This framework shifts focus from judging the event alone to understanding the resources required to restore a person’s capacity for ordinary living.

## Human signal

The emphasis moves from comparing suffering to restoring freedom and ease.
