## Core idea

Cultural storytelling functions as a form of public psychological infrastructure that shapes how people perceive risk, trust, and human nature.

## Structure

Fear-driven media amplifies rare dangers and portrays others as inherently threatening. This primes chronic vigilance, reduces empathy, and normalizes punitive responses to conflict. When societies repeatedly consume narratives of hidden predators and constant danger, people internalize a worldview that expects hostility. A healthier cultural ecosystem treats media as a public health tool that models cooperation, boundary negotiation, repair processes, and collective problem solving. Stories can generate drama and tension without dehumanizing characters or framing conflict as proof of inherent evil.

## Human signal

Narratives begin strengthening the social brain rather than exhausting it.
