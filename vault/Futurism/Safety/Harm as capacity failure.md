## Core idea

Harm is better understood not as evidence of malicious individuals but as a signal that human needs collided in systems lacking sufficient emotional, relational, or structural capacity.

## Structure

Traditional moral frameworks search for a culprit whose wrongdoing explains the event. A capacity-based perspective instead examines whether the people involved had the skills, regulation, support, or social structures necessary to meet their needs without collision. When emotional load exceeds relational capacity, harmful outcomes can emerge even when no one intends injury. The analytic focus therefore shifts from identifying bad actors to identifying missing capacities: emotional literacy, support networks, environmental safety, or relational skills.

## Human signal

This perspective softens moral condemnation without denying real impact. It treats harm as a systems signal rather than a personal defect.
