## Core idea

These circles provide the small-scale social fabric religion often supplies: a place where people are known, supported, expected, and accountable without being subordinated to authority.

## Structure

Large institutions alone cannot generate the intimacy required for sustained belonging. Religion often solved this through parish structures, study groups, and congregational micro-networks where people’s absences, needs, and behavior were visible. The secular analogue is a system of small circles, typically six to ten people, matched by location and preference but not reduced to affinity branding. These groups meet regularly to ask practical and moral questions: what do you need, what can you offer, what are you trying to follow through on, where are you struggling, and what service can we do together. This creates a lightweight but real web of recognition. A person is not only a participant in a public institution; they are someone others can notice, support, and challenge.

The accountability element is important because meaning without responsibility becomes vagueness. Yet the anti-harm design is equally important because accountability can become domination. There is no confession to a superior, no extraction of intimate information for status or leverage, no public shaming as spectacle, and no purity tribunal. When harm occurs, there is an optional repair process grounded in consent, boundaries, and, where needed, skilled third-party mediation. The emphasis is on restoring integrity and safety, not producing submission or humiliation.

## Human signal

The desired feeling is that you matter enough to be known, but not in a way that lets anyone own your conscience.
