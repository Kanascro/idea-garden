## Core idea

If religion is analyzed functionally rather than doctrinally, it appears less as a truth-claim system and more as a bundled social technology. It provides belonging, meaning, moral vocabulary, ritual regulation of the nervous system, life-stage containers, crisis care, accountability, service, art, awe, and a place for grief. The problem is not that these functions exist; the problem is that they have historically been fused to authority, conformity pressure, identity policing, epistemic closure, and control over social life.

## Structure

The design task is therefore subtractive rather than purely innovative: preserve the functions religion performs well while removing the mechanisms by which those functions become coercive. The central harms come from meaning being monopolized, belonging being conditional, and ritual being used as a compliance technology. A viable secular substitute must distribute power, normalize exit, prevent charismatic capture, and refuse any monopoly on moral legitimacy. It must also avoid capitalist distortion, because once existential needs become products, care is reorganized around retention, branding, and dependency rather than public good. The proposal is not “abolish religion and leave a vacuum,” but “rebuild the useful layers as public commons.”

## Human signal

This frame takes seriously that people do not only need services. They need places where suffering can be held, transitions can be witnessed, and existence can feel shared without being ruled.
