## Core idea

People do not only need support in ordinary time; they also need periodic exits from ordinary tempo. The challenge is to provide that without creating an authority who owns transcendence.

## Structure

Sacred time is a social technology for interrupting the endlessness of routine. It marks certain hours or days as qualitatively different: slower, quieter, more reflective, more symbolic, less instrumental. In religious settings this difference often derives from metaphysical framing and clerical authority. A secular system can preserve the temporal distinction without the ontological monopoly. A digital sabbath, a community vigil, a silent morning, or a pilgrimage walk can all create a break in tempo that reorients perception and value. The person returns to ordinary life with a changed relation to time, not because an institution declared cosmic permission, but because a shared container made the shift possible.

What must be avoided is allowing those who curate depth to become owners of depth. The facilitators of sacred time are hosts, not interpreters of ultimate reality. Their task is to maintain the conditions under which slowness, reflection, and awe can occur. That distinction keeps the experience powerful without making it politically dangerous.

## Human signal

A society should be able to offer pause that feels profound without demanding surrender in exchange.
