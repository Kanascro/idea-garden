## Core idea

Any institution built to meet existential, relational, and ritual needs becomes dangerous when tied to growth, branding, or engagement incentives. Meaning infrastructure must be decommodified to remain trustworthy.

## Structure

Capitalist incentives corrupt these functions in specific ways. If belonging becomes a product, retention matters more than freedom. If grief becomes a market, emotional dependence becomes monetizable. If ritual becomes content, spectacle outcompetes depth. If moral language becomes branding, institutions begin optimizing for visibility rather than integrity. This is why public funding and strict transparency are not side details but constitutive design features. Budgets, donations, and influence channels must be visible. Donations caps prevent wealthy patrons from converting generosity into governance power. There should be no engagement optimization metrics analogous to platform addiction logic.

Decommodification changes the institution’s emotional posture. It does not need to keep people activated, anxious, loyal, or enthralled in order to survive. It can afford to let participation ebb and flow. It can remain boring where boring is kind, gentle where gentleness is enough, and spacious where sales logic would demand stickiness.

## Human signal

People can finally tell the difference between being cared for and being cultivated as a resource.
