## Core idea

Any secular replacement for religion needs structural safeguards that function like building codes. Good intentions are insufficient because the same human needs that make these institutions necessary also make them vulnerable to capture.

## Structure

The first rule is no unquestionable authority. Facilitation must rotate, governance must be transparent, and community oversight must be real rather than symbolic. The second rule is no monopoly on belonging. People should be encouraged to have multiple communities so that no institution can hold their entire social world hostage. The third is no shame-based compliance: no purity norms, fear-of-exile cultures, moral panics, or emotional blackmail disguised as care. The fourth is that consent and exit are sacred. Leaving must be easy, uninterrogated, and free of guilt scripts, because freedom to depart is what distinguishes support from capture. The fifth is epistemic humility: teach people how to think, compare, and question rather than what to believe. The sixth is repair over punishment: conflict systems should aim at safety, boundaries, and restoration rather than ritualized condemnation. The seventh is no profit motive: public funding, capped donations, and transparent budgets prevent existential needs from being monetized into retention strategies.

These are not decorative values. They are defensive architecture against the predictable failure modes of any meaning-making institution. Humans generate attachment, hierarchy, dependency, and prestige quickly. A viable commons must therefore assume corruption pressure from the start and design accordingly.

## Human signal

This is the sober part of the vision: care requires trust, but trust without guardrails curdles into control faster than idealists admit.
