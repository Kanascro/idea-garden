## Core idea

A core anti-harm principle is that no single institution should be allowed to become a person’s total social universe. Plural attachment is a safeguard against domination.

## Structure

Many religious harms intensify because the institution is not merely one community among others. It is family, friendship network, moral authority, service hub, dating pool, crisis support, childcare web, and source of identity all at once. Under those conditions, dissent becomes existentially expensive. To resist this, a secular commons must actively normalize multiple affiliations. People should belong to circles, clubs, neighborhoods, artistic groups, political formations, and other communities alongside the sanctuary system. The institution should celebrate porousness rather than exclusivity.

This matters because dependency magnifies coercion even in the absence of explicit abuse. When one place is your only place, the threat of loss distorts consent. By encouraging diversified belonging, the commons makes exit materially and emotionally survivable. It thereby prevents care from becoming hostage-taking.

## Human signal

Freedom feels different when leaving one room does not mean losing the whole world.
