## Core idea

A humane secular community needs real conflict processes, but those processes must be oriented toward repair, boundary clarity, and safety rather than moral spectacle.

## Structure

Religious communities often maintain order through shame, confession, and expulsion. Secular spaces sometimes swing to the opposite failure mode: avoidance until resentment hardens, or public denunciation rituals that reproduce the same punitive energy in different language. Accountability circles attempt a middle path. Harm is neither ignored nor dramatized into community theater. There are agreed procedures for naming impact, seeking mediation, setting boundaries, clarifying conditions for repair, and determining when distance rather than reconciliation is necessary. The process is structured, not improvised under emotional pressure.

This design matters because a community without conflict process quickly becomes unsafe, but a community whose conflict process becomes a prestige arena becomes predatory in a different way. Repair must therefore be principled, consent-based, and proportionate. Its aim is not to purify the collective through punishment but to maintain trust and workable coexistence. The institution teaches that accountability is part of belonging, but belonging is not conditional on public self-abasement.

## Human signal

There is dignity in being able to say “harm happened” without the whole room turning into a courtroom or a mob.
