## Core idea

Interface design is not only about function or clarity. It also communicates an emotional atmosphere that can either settle or agitate the nervous system. For sensitive users, tonal design is structural, not cosmetic.

## Structure

This nervous system responds strongly to softness, slowness, kindness, non-invasive cues, visual quiet, and emotional spaciousness. That means interfaces should be calm but not cold, warm but not childish, minimal but not sterile, poetic but not whimsical. The tone should evoke something like a Japanese tea house rather than a casino, mall, or factory. This is not a stylistic preference layered on top of usability. Tonality governs whether the user feels respected, hurried, overstimulated, patronized, or held. A soft interface can still be precise. A quiet interface can still be alive. The challenge is to create atmospheres that support dignity and presence without flattening the experience into beige emptiness or sentimental coziness. Emotional tonality becomes part of the ethics of the interface.

## Human signal

The user is not only asking what the system does. They are asking what kind of feeling-world it asks them to live inside.
